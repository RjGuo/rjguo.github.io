Valleytronics in Two-dimensional systems

Valleytronics in Two-dimensional systems

Valley

In analogy to charge for electronics and spin for spintronics, valley, as a new degree of freedom for electrons, can be utilized to encode and process information, which is characterized as Valleytronics and has drawn considerable attention due to its significant potential for developing next-generation minimized devices.

The valley refers to a local energy minimum (maximum) in conduction (valence) band at the K and K’ points in the first Brillouin Zone, which is robust against phonon and defect scatterings due to the large separation in the momentum space. Therefore, two-dimensional materials with hexagonal lattice should be the most competitive candidates to be applied in valleytronic devices.

Valley polarization

In order to distinguish non-equivalent valleys, the major challenage in two-dimensional valley materials is to break the degeneracy between the valleys, thus forming the valley polarization, which opens a window for capturing the valley degree of freedom and is indispensable for valleytronic applications. The degeneracy between valleys results in the protections of inversion symmetry and time-reversal symmetry. Therefore, monolayers of 2H-phase transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) are the most promising candidates for valley polarization, since the inversion symmetry for their structures is explicitly broken, and noncentrosymmetry together with intrinsic spin-orbit coupling (SOC) derived from the d orbitals of heavy transition metals lead spin and valley degree of freedom couple strongly. As a result, in TMDs monolayers, it is very fundamental and effective to introduce an external magnetic field to break the time-reversal symmetry and induce valley splitting. Unfortunately, the extreme field strength for a sizable valley splitting is not accessible in practice use. To explore intrinsic valley polarization in TMDs is thus highly desirable.

Perspective (from electron mobility)

There has been a number of comprehensive discussions towards the future perspectives of information processing and other practical applications of valleytronics. Here I would like to give a viewpoint from electron mobility. As you may know, electron mobility can be affacted by electron inter-valley scatterings, which originate from the same energy level of the valleys. However, single-valley states, which intrinsically holds only one valley and thus robust valley-polarized physics, was discovered in valleytronics research. This single-valley feature can block or suppress the inter-valley scatterings, especially the scatterings caused by electron-defect interactions, which may increase the electron mobility in these materials.
